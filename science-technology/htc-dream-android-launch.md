# HTC Dream and the Launch of Android

**Category**: Science & Technology
**Key figures**: Andy Rubin (co-founder of Android Inc. and head of Android at Google), Google Inc. (platform developer), the Open Handset Alliance (industry consortium), HTC (device manufacturer), T-Mobile (launch carrier)

## Summary

On September 23, 2008, HTC and the carrier T-Mobile announced the HTC Dream, the first commercially available mobile phone to run Google's Android operating system. Marketed in the United States as the T-Mobile G1, it went on sale on October 22, 2008 for US$179 with a two-year service contract. The device shipped with Android 1.0 and combined a 3.2-inch capacitive touchscreen (320×480 resolution) with a slide-out physical QWERTY keyboard and a clickable trackball for navigation. Its hardware included a 528 MHz Qualcomm MSM7201A processor, 192 MB of RAM, 256 MB of internal storage expandable via microSD card, and a 3.15-megapixel autofocus camera.

Android itself originated outside Google: Android Inc. was founded in 2003 and was acquired by Google in 2005. On November 5, 2007, Google announced the Open Handset Alliance — a consortium of mobile operators, handset makers, and chip manufacturers including T-Mobile, HTC, Motorola, Qualcomm, Texas Instruments, and Samsung — formed to develop and promote Android as a free, open-source platform supporting third-party applications. The HTC Dream was the first product to bring that effort to consumers, positioning Android against Apple's iPhone, Research in Motion's BlackBerry, and Symbian.

The G1 integrated Google's online services directly into the handset, including Gmail with push email, Google Maps, Search, YouTube, and Talk, and a WebKit-based browser. It also introduced the Android Market, an on-device storefront from which users could download third-party applications — the open counterpart to Apple's App Store, which had opened that July. Uptake was steady: T-Mobile reported that it had sold more than one million G1 units in the United States by April 2009.

## Significance

The HTC Dream marked the public debut of Android, which grew from this single device into the most widely used mobile operating system in the world, running on billions of phones and tablets across hundreds of manufacturers. Its open-source, licensable model — any manufacturer could build hardware around the platform — contrasted sharply with Apple's integrated single-vendor approach and established the central competitive dynamic of the smartphone era. The Android Market introduced with the G1 evolved into Google Play, one of the two dominant channels of the global app economy. Although the Dream's slide-out keyboard and trackball were quickly superseded by all-touchscreen designs, its 2008 launch is regarded as a pivotal moment in mobile computing, seeding an ecosystem that would reshape the telecommunications, software, and consumer-electronics industries over the following decade.
