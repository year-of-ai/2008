# 2008 Mumbai Terrorist Attacks

**Category**: History & Politics
**Key figures**: Ajmal Amir Kasab, David Coleman Headley, Hafiz Saeed, Hemant Karkare, Manmohan Singh, Pranab Mukherjee

## Summary

The 2008 Mumbai terrorist attacks — commonly referred to in India as 26/11 — were a series of coordinated shooting and bombing attacks carried out by ten militants affiliated with the Pakistan-based Islamist organization Lashkar-e-Taiba. Beginning at approximately 21:30 IST on November 26, 2008, and continuing until the morning of November 29, the attacks struck multiple high-profile targets across South Mumbai simultaneously, paralyzing the city of nearly eighteen million people for approximately sixty hours.

The attackers departed Karachi by sea on November 21, 2008, aboard a Pakistani cargo vessel. On November 23, the group forcibly boarded the Indian fishing trawler Kuber, killing its crew except for the captain, who navigated them toward the Mumbai coast. On the evening of November 26, approximately four miles offshore, the captain was killed and the ten men transferred to inflatable dinghies to reach land. They then split into smaller groups and fanned out across South Mumbai to their pre-assigned targets.

The sites attacked included Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Terminus (CST), a major railway hub where gunmen opened fire on crowds; Leopold Cafe, a popular restaurant frequented by foreigners; the Taj Mahal Palace Hotel; the Oberoi Trident Hotel; Nariman House, a Jewish community center (Chabad house); Cama Hospital; and several other locations. The sieges at the Taj and Oberoi hotels and Nariman House proved the most prolonged, lasting until the final morning. India's National Security Guard (NSG), MARCOS (Marine Commandos), and Rapid Action Force were deployed; the NSG rescued approximately 250 people from the Oberoi, 300 from the Taj, and 60 from Nariman House. Nine of the ten attackers were killed by security forces. The sole survivor, Ajmal Amir Kasab, was captured alive by the Mumbai Police.

Kasab's arrest provided Indian investigators with direct evidence linking the operation to Lashkar-e-Taiba and to handlers in Pakistan. His confession and subsequent trial revealed the extent of the planning, which had involved years of reconnaissance. American-Pakistani operative David Coleman Headley made five surveillance trips to Mumbai between September 2006 and July 2008, filming prospective targets and meeting with Lashkar members and Pakistani Inter-Services Intelligence (ISI) handlers after each visit. Headley's ISI handler, identified as Major Iqbal, provided $25,000 to fund the reconnaissance trips. In 2013, a US federal judge sentenced Headley to 35 years in prison for his role in the plot.

The total death toll reached 166 civilians and security personnel (excluding the nine attackers killed), with more than 300 others injured. Among the dead were 29 foreign nationals and prominent police officers including Anti-Terrorism Squad chief Hemant Karkare, encounter specialist Vijay Salaskar, and Additional Commissioner of Police Ashok Kamte, all killed in the initial hours of the attack. Kasab was convicted of murder and waging war against India; he was sentenced to death in May 2010 and executed at Yerwada Central Jail on November 21, 2012.

## Significance

The Mumbai attacks were among the deadliest terrorist attacks in Indian history and forced a fundamental reassessment of the country's counterterrorism architecture. In the aftermath, India created the National Investigation Agency (NIA), its first dedicated federal agency for investigating terrorism offenses, and passed the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Amendment Act 2008, which strengthened the legal framework for prosecuting terrorism. The attacks also prompted a broad review of coastal security, intelligence coordination, and the command structure for emergency response in major cities.

Diplomatically, the attacks severely strained India-Pakistan relations, which had been cautiously improving through the Composite Dialogue process. India suspended the dialogue, demanded Pakistan dismantle Lashkar-e-Taiba, and handed Islamabad a list of 20 most-wanted individuals. India's External Affairs Minister at the time raised the possibility of military strikes against terrorist camps in Pakistan. Pakistan denied state involvement, and relations remained tense for years. The episode deepened international scrutiny of Pakistan's relationship with non-state militant groups, particularly in the context of the broader US-led war on terrorism in the region.

The attacks also reshaped global counterterrorism doctrine. The use of a small, well-trained team deploying low-cost conventional weapons — rifles and grenades — to hold a major metropolis hostage for nearly three days demonstrated the vulnerability of open urban environments and influenced security planning in cities worldwide. The event came to serve as a reference case in urban warfare studies, intelligence-sharing frameworks, and the debate over state-sponsored terrorism, given the accumulated evidence of Pakistani military intelligence's links to Lashkar-e-Taiba's leadership.
