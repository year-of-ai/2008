# 2008 US Presidential Debates

**Category**: History & Politics  
**Key figures**: Barack Obama (Democratic nominee), John McCain (Republican nominee), Joe Biden (Democratic VP nominee), Sarah Palin (Republican VP nominee), moderators Jim Lehrer, Gwen Ifill, Tom Brokaw, Bob Schieffer

## Summary

The 2008 United States presidential election featured three general-election debates between Democratic nominee Senator Barack Obama of Illinois and Republican nominee Senator John McCain of Arizona, plus one vice-presidential debate. The debate series was organized by the Commission on Presidential Debates (CPD) and broadcast across all major television networks and online. The debates took place against the backdrop of the acute phase of the [2008 financial crisis](../society-economics/2008-financial-crisis.md) — Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy three days before the first presidential debate, and Congress was debating the [TARP bailout](../society-economics/tarp-bailout.md) during the intervening weeks — making economic policy the dominant theme throughout.

## First Presidential Debate — September 26, 2008

**Venue**: University of Mississippi, Oxford, Mississippi  
**Moderator**: Jim Lehrer (PBS *NewsHour*)  
**Theme**: Foreign policy and national security (format adapted in real time to include the economy)

The first debate attracted approximately **52.4 million television viewers**; held on a Friday evening, it drew a smaller audience than the opening 2004 Bush–Kerry debate (about 62.5 million). The timing was extraordinary: Lehman Brothers had filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy on September 15, and markets were in turmoil. Senator McCain had dramatically "suspended" his campaign on September 24, threatening to cancel the debate to return to Washington for bailout negotiations — a gambit that drew criticism as a political stunt when he ultimately appeared on stage.

Obama presented a measured, professorial persona and repeatedly linked McCain's positions to the policies of the unpopular George W. Bush administration. McCain attacked Obama's foreign policy judgment, particularly regarding the surge in Iraq, and sought to portray him as naive on national security. Post-debate polling by CNN showed Obama winning the debate 51% to 38% among registered voters who watched.

## Vice-Presidential Debate — October 2, 2008

**Venue**: Washington University in St. Louis, Missouri  
**Moderator**: Gwen Ifill (PBS)  
**Viewership**: Approximately **69.9 million** — the most-watched vice-presidential debate in US history at that time

The VP debate between Senator Joe Biden (Delaware) and Alaska Governor Sarah Palin drew the largest debate audience of the cycle. Palin had given stumbling interviews to CBS's Katie Couric in the preceding week — particularly failing to name newspapers she read or to articulate the legal basis for executive privilege — raising questions about her readiness. The debate format, negotiated by the McCain campaign, limited follow-up questions.

Palin performed better than lowered expectations, delivering rehearsed answers with energy and colloquial appeal ("You betcha," "Say it ain't so, Joe"). However, she did not answer several specific questions directly, instead pivoting to prepared talking points. Biden combined empathy with command of policy detail, most notably in an emotional moment recounting the loss of his first wife and daughter in a 1972 car accident. Post-debate CNN polling showed Biden winning 51% to 36%, though analysts noted Palin had stabilized the Republican ticket's morale.

## Second Presidential Debate — October 7, 2008

**Venue**: Belmont University, Nashville, Tennessee  
**Moderator**: Tom Brokaw (NBC)  
**Format**: Town hall (questions from audience members)

The second debate used a town-hall format in which undecided voters posed questions directly. The format was widely viewed as favorable to McCain, who had a reputation for town-hall effectiveness — indeed, he had proposed holding ten weekly joint town halls with Obama over the summer (Obama declined). In the event, Obama handled the format comfortably, pacing the stage and addressing questioners by name.

A notable moment came when McCain referred to Obama as "that one" — a phrase widely interpreted as dismissive and which generated significant media commentary on tone and respect. The debate attracted approximately **63.2 million** viewers. Post-debate polling again showed Obama ahead: CNN registered 54% to 30% in Obama's favor among debate watchers.

## Third Presidential Debate — October 15, 2008

**Venue**: Hofstra University, Hempstead, New York  
**Moderator**: Bob Schieffer (CBS *Face the Nation*)  
**Viewership**: Approximately **56.5 million**

The third and final debate, conducted at a table with the two candidates seated across from each other, was the most contentious of the three. McCain introduced "Joe the Plumber" — Joe Wurzelbacher of Ohio, with whom Obama had spoken at a campaign event — as a symbolic small business owner who would be harmed by Obama's tax plan. Obama and McCain exchanged this reference dozens of times, and "Joe the Plumber" became a cultural shorthand for the debate over tax policy and income redistribution.

McCain attempted to link Obama to domestic radical William Ayers, a controversial figure from the Weather Underground, seeking to raise character and association concerns. Obama parried these attacks calmly, declining to be drawn into an exchange on Ayers. Post-debate CNN polling showed Obama winning 58% to 31% — his largest margin across the three debates.

## Significance

The 2008 debate series was consequential in shaping the final weeks of the [2008 presidential election campaign](../history-politics/barack-obama-election.md). Obama entered the first debate trailing or tied with McCain in most national polls; by mid-October he had opened leads of 5–10 percentage points in national surveys and in key swing states including Pennsylvania, Ohio, and Florida.

Several dynamics distinguished the 2008 debates as historically significant:

**Economic context**: No modern debate series had been framed so acutely by an ongoing financial emergency. The [2008 financial crisis](../society-economics/2008-financial-crisis.md) — including the [Lehman Brothers collapse](../society-economics/lehman-brothers-collapse.md) and the TARP debate — forced both candidates to address complex economic policy in real time. Obama's composure in this environment reinforced his message of calm leadership.

**Historic candidacy**: Obama became the first African American nominee of a major party to participate in a presidential debate, a milestone noted by commentators and acknowledged by many viewers.

**Digital amplification**: The 2008 debates were the first presidential debates to be substantially discussed and dissected in real time via social media platforms — then primarily Facebook (founded 2004) and Twitter (founded 2006, but growing rapidly in 2008). Debate "fact-checking" emerged as a distinct media activity, practiced by news organizations live during and immediately after the debates.

**Palin effect**: The VP debate's record viewership reflected both anticipation and anxiety about Palin, whose selection by McCain had initially energized conservative voters but whose subsequent media appearances created doubts about her qualifications.
