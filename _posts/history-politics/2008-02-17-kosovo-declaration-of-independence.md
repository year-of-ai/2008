## Summary

On February 17, 2008, the Assembly of Kosovo, meeting in the capital Pristina, adopted a declaration proclaiming the Republic of Kosovo to be an independent and sovereign state. The measure passed with the support of 109 of the assembly's 120 members; the 11 deputies representing Kosovo's Serb minority boycotted the session. Prime Minister Hashim Thaçi and President Fatmir Sejdiu led the proceedings, and the declaration framed the new state's independence as conforming to the settlement plan drafted by United Nations Special Envoy Martti Ahtisaari.

The declaration ended nearly nine years of international administration that had governed the province since the 1999 Kosovo War under UN Security Council Resolution 1244, while Kosovo had remained formally part of Serbia. It accepted continued international supervision, committed the new state to protections for its ethnic minorities, and renounced union with any other country. Serbia, supported by Russia, immediately condemned the declaration as an illegal violation of its territorial integrity, and the event split the international community: the United States and major Western European powers extended recognition within days, while Russia, China, and a number of other states — including several European Union members facing their own separatist movements — refused to do so.

## Key Facts

- **Date and place:** February 17, 2008, at a session of the Assembly of Kosovo in Pristina
- **Vote:** adopted by 109 of 120 assembly members; the 11 ethnic-Serb deputies boycotted the session, while representatives of other minorities took part
- **Status before independence:** administered since June 1999 by the UN Interim Administration Mission in Kosovo (UNMIK) under Security Council Resolution 1244, while remaining nominally a province of Serbia
- **Legal framework:** the declaration invoked the Comprehensive Proposal for the Kosovo Status Settlement (the "Ahtisaari Plan"), which envisaged supervised independence with extensive minority safeguards after status talks deadlocked in late 2007
- **International supervision:** an International Civilian Office oversaw implementation; the European Union deployed the EULEX rule-of-law mission, and the NATO-led Kosovo Force (KFOR) provided security
- **United States recognition:** February 18, 2008 — one day after the declaration — announced by Secretary of State Condoleezza Rice on behalf of President George W. Bush
- **Serbian and Russian response:** Serbia declared the act illegal and recalled ambassadors from recognizing states; Russia called for an emergency UN Security Council session, held the same day, where members were sharply divided
- **International Court of Justice:** at Serbia's request (October 8, 2008), the UN General Assembly sought an advisory opinion; on July 22, 2010, the ICJ found that the declaration "did not violate general international law"
- **Recognition:** by the end of the decade roughly 110 UN member states had recognized Kosovo; notable non-recognizers included Serbia, Russia, China, Spain, Greece, Cyprus, Romania, and Slovakia

## Background

Kosovo, a territory with an ethnic-Albanian majority and a Serb minority, holds deep historical and religious significance for Serbs. After the suppression of its autonomy under Slobodan Milošević and the ensuing conflict between Serbian forces and the Kosovo Liberation Army, NATO's 1999 air campaign drove Serbian forces out and placed the province under international administration through UN Resolution 1244. That resolution left Kosovo's final status unresolved, affirming Serbian sovereignty in principle while establishing self-government in practice.

UN-led status negotiations under Martti Ahtisaari produced a 2007 plan for "supervised independence," but Russia signalled it would veto any Security Council endorsement, and talks between Belgrade and Pristina collapsed without agreement. With the status process at an impasse, Kosovo's leadership moved to declare independence unilaterally, in coordination with the United States and leading EU governments, while pledging to implement the Ahtisaari Plan's provisions.

## International Reaction

Recognition followed quickly from the United States and large parts of the European Union — including the United Kingdom, France, Germany, and Italy — and from many other states. The EU did not adopt a common position, however: Spain, Greece, Cyprus, Romania, and Slovakia withheld recognition, several of them wary of encouraging secessionist claims at home. Serbia rejected the declaration outright, recalled ambassadors from recognizing capitals, and pursued legal and diplomatic countermeasures, while continuing to administer Serb-majority areas in northern Kosovo.

Russia and China, both permanent members of the Security Council, opposed the declaration as a breach of Serbian sovereignty and a dangerous precedent for unilateral secession. The dispute remained unresolved by the Security Council, and Serbia turned instead to the International Court of Justice, whose 2010 advisory opinion held that the declaration itself did not contravene international law without settling the broader question of statehood.

## Significance

**The last major state to emerge from the breakup of Yugoslavia** — Kosovo's declaration marked the final act in the violent dissolution of the former Yugoslav federation, redrawing the political map of the Western Balkans nearly two decades after the process began.

**A contested precedent for self-determination** — The case sharpened a long-running debate between the principles of territorial integrity and self-determination. Supporters cast Kosovo as a unique instance arising from grave human-rights abuses and prolonged international administration; opponents, led by Russia, argued it set a precedent for unilateral secession — a framing Moscow later invoked in justifying its actions in Georgia, Crimea, and Ukraine.

**A divided international order** — The split between recognizing and non-recognizing states, including disunity within the European Union, illustrated the limits of great-power consensus and left Kosovo outside the United Nations, with its status a persistent point of friction in Balkan and great-power diplomacy.
