## Summary

On September 4, 2008, Arizona Republican Senator John McCain formally accepted the Republican Party's presidential nomination at the Xcel Energy Center in St. Paul, Minnesota, delivering a 48-minute speech before approximately 20,000 delegates. His address emphasized his biography — 23 years of military service, five and a half years as a prisoner of war in Hanoi's "Hanoi Hilton" after his A-4 Skyhawk was shot down over North Vietnam in October 1967 — and positioned him as a "Maverick" reformer willing to challenge both parties. The Republican National Convention had been shortened from four days to three due to Hurricane Gustav threatening the Gulf Coast, a symbolic echo of the Hurricane Katrina response that had damaged Republicans in 2005.

McCain had secured the Republican nomination through a remarkable political resurrection. After his campaign nearly collapsed in mid-2007 due to fundraising shortfalls and staff departures, he pursued a stripped-down "No Surrender" approach in New Hampshire — holding over 100 town halls — and won the January 8 primary with 37.1% of the vote. Subsequent wins in South Carolina (January 19, 33.2%) and Florida (January 29, 36.0%) made him the de facto nominee, mathematically clinching the nomination on March 4 after victories in Texas, Ohio, Rhode Island, and Vermont.

## The Palin Decision

The most consequential and controversial choice of the McCain campaign was the selection of Alaska Governor Sarah Palin as his vice-presidential running mate, announced on August 29, 2008 — the day after Barack Obama's historic acceptance speech in Denver's Invesco Field drew 84,000 attendees. Palin, 44, had served as Alaska's governor for less than two years and was previously the mayor of Wasilla (population ~9,000). She was the first woman ever nominated for vice president by the Republican Party, and only the second woman nominated for VP by either major party (after Geraldine Ferraro in 1984).

The announcement generated an immediate "Palin Bounce" in polling: Gallup surveys showed McCain briefly leading Obama 49–44% in early September, up from a deficit of several points. Palin's convention speech, watched by 37.2 million viewers (more than Obama's Denver speech), delivered sharp attacks on Obama's record and became a viral phenomenon. However, subsequent media scrutiny raised questions about her foreign policy knowledge: a September interview with CBS News anchor Katie Couric, in which Palin struggled to name newspapers she read or Supreme Court decisions beyond Roe v. Wade, caused lasting damage. Polls showed voter confidence in Palin as VP material drop from 52% to 38% between September and October.

## The Financial Crisis Response

The 2008 financial crisis fundamentally reshaped the election's trajectory. On September 15, Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy — the largest bankruptcy in U.S. history at the time — triggering a global market panic. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 504 points (4.4%) that day alone, and the credit markets seized. McCain's September 15 statement that "the fundamentals of our economy are strong" — made the same morning as the Lehman collapse — was seized upon by the Obama campaign as evidence of economic disconnection, and it became one of the most replayed clips of the campaign.

On September 24, McCain announced he was "suspending" his campaign to return to Washington to help broker the $700 billion Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP), calling for the first presidential debate (scheduled September 26 in Oxford, Mississippi) to be postponed. Obama rejected the postponement, and the debate proceeded as planned. McCain appeared erratic to many voters — polls showed Americans preferred Obama's steadier response by margins of 18–24 points on economic leadership. The TARP bill initially failed in the House on September 29 (with McCain voting for the revised version that passed October 3), and the stock market fell 777 points on the day of the first House rejection.

## Campaign Strategy and Messaging

McCain's campaign cycled through several distinct strategic phases:

- **Spring 2008**: The "Experience vs. Change" frame, emphasizing McCain's Senate tenure and military record against Obama's brief national career.
- **August**: The "Celebrity" attack ads (comparing Obama to Paris Hilton and Britney Spears for his cult-of-personality following) and the "The One" satire — the most viewed political ads of the cycle, though their effect on persuadable voters was ambiguous.
- **October**: "Joe the Plumber" — following a September 26 Obama exchange with Ohio plumber Samuel "Joe" Wurzelbacher about a small business tax question, McCain mentioned "Joe the Plumber" 21 times in the October 15 third presidential debate, making Wurzelbacher a national figure and proxy for small-business tax concerns.
- **Late October**: Sustained attacks on Obama's association with former Weather Underground member William Ayers, which prompted some Republicans to worry that the campaign was "going negative" without an economic contrast message.

## The Presidential Debates

The [three presidential debates](/news/history-politics/2008-presidential-debates/) between McCain and Obama drew historically large television audiences:

| Date | Location | Viewers |
|------|-----------|---------|
| September 26 | Ole Miss, Oxford, MS | 52.4 million |
| October 7 | Belmont University, Nashville, TN | 63.2 million |
| October 15 | Hofstra University, Hempstead, NY | 56.5 million |

The October 7 "town hall" format, held at Belmont University in Nashville, was widely considered McCain's best potential venue given his mastery of the format. However, his movement around the stage — including a widely-noted moment where he gestured toward Obama saying "that one" — generated negative commentary. Post-debate polls consistently showed Obama winning all three encounters by margins of 8–15 percentage points.

## Election Results and Concession

On November 4, 2008, McCain received 59,948,323 popular votes (45.7%) and 173 electoral votes, losing to [Obama's 365 electoral votes](/news/history-politics/barack-obama-election/) and 52.9% of the popular vote. McCain lost nine states that George W. Bush had carried in 2004, including Florida, Ohio, Indiana, Virginia, and North Carolina. His campaign spent approximately $333 million, compared to Obama's record-breaking $750 million.

McCain's concession speech in Phoenix, Arizona, delivered at 11:00 PM Eastern Time on election night, was widely praised across partisan lines for its grace and patriotism. When audience members booed at the mention of Obama's name, McCain gestured for silence and said: "I urge all Americans who supported me to join me in not just congratulating him but offering our next president our goodwill and earnest effort to find ways to come together." His line addressing a woman who had called Obama "an Arab" — "No, ma'am. He's a decent family man and citizen that I just happen to have disagreements with on fundamental issues" — became one of the most-quoted moments of the election.

## Significance

McCain's 2008 campaign had legacies that extended well beyond its defeat. The selection of Sarah Palin introduced to national prominence a strand of conservative populism that reshaped the Republican Party's base appeal through the Tea Party movement (2009–2010) and beyond. Her style — plain-spoken, culturally conservative, skeptical of elite institutions — prefigured political figures who rose in her wake. McCain's insistence on campaign-trail civility toward Obama — refusing to use Jeremiah Wright associations, correcting supporters who called Obama "Arab" or "terrorist" — established a norm of basic dignity that he was praised for by historians and observers across the political spectrum.

McCain's loss also illuminated structural Republican vulnerabilities in a changing electorate: his margins among white voters were insufficient to offset Obama's coalitional advantages among Black, Latino, young, and college-educated voters. The campaign's internal debates — between the "experience" and "change" frames, between economic populism and traditional conservatism — anticipated the identity tensions that would define Republican politics for the following decade.
