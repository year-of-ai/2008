## Summary

Twilight premiered on November 21, 2008, adapted from Stephenie Meyer's 2005 bestselling novel of the same name. Directed by Catherine Hardwicke with a screenplay by Melissa Rosenberg, the film follows seventeen-year-old Bella Swan (Kristen Stewart) as she relocates to the remote town of Forks, Washington, where she encounters Edward Cullen (Robert Pattinson), a member of a family of vegetarian vampires. As Bella and Edward's romance deepens, they face danger when nomadic vampire James (Cam Gigandet) targets Bella, forcing Edward and his family to protect her and escalate their commitment.

The film achieved $69.6 million in its opening weekend across 3,419 theaters, setting the record for the highest opening ever for a female-directed film. It accumulated $192.8 million domestically and $214.4 million internationally for a worldwide total of approximately $407–412 million — an extraordinary return on its modest $37 million production budget. Critical reception was mixed (Rotten Tomatoes 48%, Metacritic 56/100), but audiences awarded it a CinemaScore of A−. The 2009 DVD release became that year's best-selling title, selling over 3 million units on its first day alone.

## Key Facts

- **Release date:** November 21, 2008 (wide release across North America)
- **Production budget:** approximately $37 million (financed and distributed by Summit Entertainment)
- **Opening weekend:** $69.6 million across 3,419 theaters — the highest opening ever for a female-directed film at the time
- **Worldwide gross:** approximately $407–412 million
- **Rotten Tomatoes:** 48% critics score; CinemaScore A−
- **Source novel:** published October 5, 2005, by Little, Brown and Company — Stephenie Meyer's debut novel
- **Principal filming locations:** Kalama, Washington; Portland, Oregon; and surrounding Pacific Northwest locations (not Forks, WA itself)
- **Running time:** 122 minutes
- **Director:** Catherine Hardwicke, the first film she directed for a major studio production after Thirteen (2003) and Lords of Dogtown (2005)
- **Robert Pattinson casting:** Pattinson, previously known as Cedric Diggory in Harry Potter and the Goblet of Fire (2005), was cast in January 2008 after initial fan resistance online — an early instance of internet fandom responding to production announcements in real time

## Production & Development

Summit Entertainment, then a mid-sized independent studio, acquired the rights to Meyer's novel and green-lit Twilight after several larger studios passed. The studio had deliberately courted Meyer's existing readership — the novel had sold over 5 million copies in the United States before filming began in February 2008 — as a pre-validated audience base, a strategy that would become standard practice in subsequent YA adaptations.

Catherine Hardwicke and screenwriter Melissa Rosenberg structured the adaptation around the romance's emotional arc rather than the fantasy elements, framing the vampire mythology as a backdrop to a coming-of-age love story. Hardwicke used handheld camera work and muted, desaturated color grading to give the film a naturalistic look unusual for the genre. Composer Carter Burwell, known for his collaborations with the Coen Brothers, wrote a minimalist score that leaned on piano and strings rather than conventional genre orchestration.

Pattinson's casting proved controversial in fan communities because his physical appearance differed from widely held expectations based on the novel's descriptions of Edward Cullen. The online debate — conducted largely on fan forums and early social media — drew significant press attention and demonstrated that a devoted book readership could generate pre-release publicity regardless of the studio's marketing spend.

Filming wrapped in May 2008, with the production company completing post-production under an accelerated schedule to meet the November release window ahead of the holiday season.

## Box Office Performance & Cultural Impact

Twilight's opening weekend of $69.6 million was immediately studied as an anomaly: the film outperformed major studio productions with significantly larger budgets and more established stars, with no male stars over 30 in leading roles and a director with no prior blockbuster experience. Its audience demographic — predominantly female, aged 13–34 — demonstrated that this segment could drive record-setting openings without the male-skewing audiences that studios had long treated as the primary blockbuster engine.

For Summit Entertainment, the film's success was transformational. The studio had financed Twilight on a modest budget and retained distribution rights; its approximately $400 million worldwide return established Summit as a viable major-studio competitor and funded the development of the remaining franchise entries. Summit was subsequently acquired by Lionsgate in January 2012 for approximately $412 million, with the Twilight franchise as the principal asset justifying the acquisition price.

The four sequels — New Moon (2009), Eclipse (2010), Breaking Dawn Part 1 (2011), and Breaking Dawn Part 2 (2012) — grossed an additional $2.9 billion worldwide, bringing the franchise's total box office to approximately $3.3 billion.

## Critical Reception

Critics were sharply divided. The majority of negative reviews faulted the script's pacing and the muted emotional register of the performances, particularly in early scenes. Positive reviews credited Hardwicke's atmospheric direction and the chemistry between Stewart and Pattinson. The 48% Rotten Tomatoes score contrasted with the A− CinemaScore, a divergence that became a frequently cited example of the gap between critical opinion and audience response for YA adaptations.

Retrospective reassessment of the film has acknowledged Hardwicke's stylistic choices — the handheld photography, the Pacific Northwest setting, the deliberate slowness of the romance — as more intentional and coherent than contemporaneous reviews credited. Several critics who initially dismissed the film have noted its influence on subsequent teen romance and fantasy productions.

## Significance

**Validated Young Adult Literary Adaptations as Blockbuster Properties** — Twilight's exceptional box office performance despite mixed critical reviews proved that young adult novels could be adapted into blockbuster films with massive commercial appeal. This success paved the way for subsequent franchises including The Hunger Games, Divergent, Percy Jackson, and The Maze Runner, fundamentally reshaping Hollywood's development slate and investment priorities for the decade that followed.

**Demonstrated Female-Directed Film Box Office Power** — The film's opening weekend record for female-directed films challenged longstanding industry assumptions about gender and commercial viability. Catherine Hardwicke's success helped legitimize female directors in major studio productions and contributed to shifting perceptions about women's capacity to direct high-budget, high-stakes entertainment.

**Illustrated the Power of Digitally-Connected Fandoms** — Twilight's exceptional performance despite mediocre critical reception highlighted the emerging influence of dedicated, internet-connected fan communities as drivers of commercial success. The passionate online fandom mobilized audiences independent of traditional critical gatekeeping, presaging how fan communities would reshape entertainment industry decision-making throughout the 2010s.

**Revitalized Vampire Romance as a Mainstream Narrative Mode** — By successfully adapting vampire mythology into a contemporary young adult romance, Twilight sparked a wave of vampire-themed television, film, and literature. The franchise redefined vampire storytelling for a generation, influencing everything from The Vampire Diaries television series to the broader paranormal romance genre.

**Launched Global Careers and a Multi-Billion-Dollar Franchise** — The film transformed Kristen Stewart and Robert Pattinson into international stars and established the foundation for a five-film franchise. The four sequels collectively grossed over $2.9 billion worldwide, with the full franchise exceeding $3.3 billion — defining young adult entertainment for a generation.
