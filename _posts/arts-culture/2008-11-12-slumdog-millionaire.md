## Summary

*Slumdog Millionaire* is a British drama film directed by Danny Boyle and co-directed by Loveleen Tandan for Indian sequences. It opened in a limited North American release on November 12, 2008, expanded to a wide United States release on January 23, 2009, and was released across the United Kingdom on January 9, 2009. The film follows Jamal Malik, an 18-year-old from the Dharavi slum district of Mumbai, who appears on the Indian version of *Who Wants to Be a Millionaire?* (*Kaun Banega Crorepati?*) and is questioned by police when he reaches the final question — authorities suspecting that someone from his background cannot have legitimately acquired such knowledge. The screenplay by Simon Beaufoy is adapted from Vikas Swarup's 2005 novel *Q & A*.

The film stars Dev Patel (then 18 years old and making his feature film debut) as Jamal, Freida Pinto as his childhood love Latika, Irrfan Khan as the police inspector, and Saurabh Shukla. The narrative structure alternates between Jamal's police interrogation in the present and a series of flashbacks — each triggered by a game-show question — tracing his childhood in poverty, his relationship with his brother Salim, and his search for Latika across the slums and criminal underworld of Mumbai.

## Production

Filming took place primarily on location in Mumbai, India, including in the Dharavi slum — one of the largest urban slums in the world, home to approximately one million people. Director Boyle worked alongside Indian co-director Loveleen Tandan, who cast the film's child actors and directed Hindi-language scenes. Child performers were recruited from the slums of Mumbai, with Ayush Mahesh Khedekar, Azharuddin Mohammed Ismail, and Rubina Ali playing younger versions of the central characters.

The film was shot digitally using handheld cameras, with Anthony Dod Mantle as cinematographer — a deliberate aesthetic choice to capture the energy and texture of Mumbai's streets. The budget was approximately $15 million (USD). Fox Searchlight Pictures acquired North American distribution rights for $5 million after the film's warm critical reception at the Telluride Film Festival in August 2008.

A.R. Rahman, the acclaimed Indian film composer, created the score — including the signature song "Jai Ho," which plays over the film's climactic Bollywood-style end-credits sequence. Rahman recorded the score in India while simultaneously working on several other Indian film projects.

## Release and Reception

*Slumdog Millionaire* premiered at the Telluride Film Festival in August 2008 and screened at the Toronto International Film Festival the same month, where it received standing ovations. It opened wide in the United States on January 23, 2009, following a limited November 2008 release in selected cities.

Critical reception was overwhelmingly positive. The film holds a 91% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on over 300 reviews. Reviewers praised its kinetic direction, emotional depth, performances (particularly from the child actors), and Rahman's score. Some Indian critics and commentators raised concerns that the film presented an overly impoverished, sensationalized image of India for Western audiences — a debate that intensified as its Oscar prospects became clear.

Commercially, the film earned $377.9 million worldwide against its $15 million production budget, making it one of the most profitable films of 2008 relative to its cost.

## Awards and Recognition

At the **81st Academy Awards** (February 22, 2009), *Slumdog Millionaire* received 10 nominations and won **8 Academy Awards**:

- Best Picture
- Best Director (Danny Boyle)
- Best Adapted Screenplay (Simon Beaufoy)
- Best Cinematography (Anthony Dod Mantle)
- Best Film Editing (Chris Dickens)
- Best Original Score (A.R. Rahman)
- Best Original Song ("Jai Ho," A.R. Rahman)
- Best Sound Mixing

The eight wins made it the most honored film of the 2008–09 awards season. A.R. Rahman won two of them — Best Original Score and Best Original Song — becoming the first Indian composer to win multiple Academy Awards in a single ceremony.

The film also won 7 BAFTA Awards (including Best Film and Best Director), 5 Critics' Choice Awards, and all four of the Golden Globe Awards for which it was nominated, including Best Motion Picture – Drama.

## Significance

*Slumdog Millionaire* marked a cultural inflection point in the global reach of Indian storytelling. Its success opened doors for Indian talent in Western film markets — A.R. Rahman's international profile expanded dramatically, leading to collaborations across Hollywood and concert tours globally. Dev Patel went on to a prominent international career; Freida Pinto similarly became a recognized face in Hollywood productions.

The film's narrative of survival, resilience, and fate ("It is written") resonated with global audiences dealing with the economic insecurity of the [2008 financial crisis]({{ '/news/society-economics/2008-financial-crisis/' | relative_url }}) — its release during the acute phase of that crisis, in late 2008 and early 2009, may have amplified its emotional appeal.

The film's depiction of Mumbai also coincided with international attention on the city following the [November 2008 Mumbai terrorist attacks]({{ '/news/history-politics/mumbai-terrorist-attacks/' | relative_url }}), keeping the city in the global consciousness and prompting audiences worldwide to engage with stories from the city's diverse communities.

Compared with other 2008 films such as [*The Dark Knight*]({{ '/news/arts-culture/the-dark-knight/' | relative_url }}) and [*WALL-E*]({{ '/news/arts-culture/wall-e/' | relative_url }}), *Slumdog Millionaire* was notable for its non-Western subject matter winning the industry's highest honor — a development that, while celebrated by many, also sparked ongoing debates about which stories get amplified and why.
