## Summary

On July 11, 2008, Apple released the iPhone 3G in 22 countries simultaneously — the first iPhone launch outside the United States. The second-generation iPhone, announced by Steve Jobs at Apple's Worldwide Developers Conference (WWDC) on June 9, 2008, introduced two transformative changes from the original: built-in 3G (HSDPA) wireless connectivity replacing the original's slower EDGE-only network, and a dramatically lower price point of $199 for the 8 GB model and $299 for the 16 GB model with a two-year carrier contract, down from the original's $499–$599 pricing.

The iPhone 3G also launched alongside iPhone OS 2.0 and the iTunes App Store — which had opened its doors the day before, on July 10, 2008, with approximately 500 applications. The combination of faster connectivity, accessible pricing, and a third-party application ecosystem fundamentally changed what a smartphone could do and who could afford one. By the end of its first weekend, Apple had sold 1 million iPhone 3G units; by year's end, more than 13.7 million had been sold globally.

## Announcement and Pre-Launch (June–July 2008)

Steve Jobs unveiled the iPhone 3G at WWDC at the Moscone Center in San Francisco on June 9, 2008, opening with what became a memorable comparison: "It's twice as fast at half the price." The WWDC keynote also announced the App Store and the iPhone SDK, which Apple had made available to developers on March 6, 2008. The six weeks between announcement and launch were marked by the rapid growth of the App Store developer ecosystem: by launch day, approximately 500 apps were available, of which roughly 125 were free. The most downloaded paid app in the App Store's first weekend was a $0.99 game called *Crash Bandicoot Nitro Kart 3D* from Vivendi Games.

Carrier partnerships were a significant part of the announcement. While the original iPhone had been exclusively tied to AT&T (then Cingular) in the U.S., Apple negotiated deals with carriers in 22 countries for the simultaneous global launch — including O2 in the UK, Rogers in Canada, Telstra in Australia, and T-Mobile in Germany. This global reach was a sharp contrast to the original iPhone's U.S.-only debut. In subsequent months, the iPhone 3G became available in over 70 countries by the end of 2008.

## Technical Specifications

The iPhone 3G's hardware represented both advances and deliberate trade-offs from its predecessor:

| Feature | iPhone (2007) | iPhone 3G (2008) |
|---------|--------------|-----------------|
| Network | EDGE (2G) | HSDPA 3G + EDGE fallback |
| GPS | No | Yes (assisted GPS) |
| Storage | 4/8/16 GB | 8/16 GB |
| Battery (talk) | 8 hours | 5 hours (3G) / 10 hours (2G) |
| Price (8 GB, contract) | $399 | $199 |
| Countries at launch | 1 (USA) | 22 |

The 3G radio was the defining addition. HSDPA theoretical download speeds of up to 3.6 Mbps (compared to EDGE's 200–300 Kbps peak) made web browsing, email with attachments, and streaming audio genuinely usable for the first time. The addition of GPS — via a SiRF Star III chip — enabled location-aware applications that would become central to the App Store economy (maps, navigation, local search). The phone ran on a Samsung-manufactured 412 MHz ARM processor and included 128 MB RAM. It lacked cut-and-paste functionality (added in iPhone OS 3.0 in 2009), MMS messaging, and support for 3rd-party background processes — limitations that critics noted but which did not dampen sales.

## Launch Day and First Weekend

Apple Stores and carrier retail locations opened at 8 AM on July 11, 2008, with lines forming in many cities the previous evening. In New York City, the line at the Fifth Avenue Apple Store stretched seven city blocks. AT&T's activation systems, which required real-time in-store activation rather than the original iPhone's at-home iTunes activation, were overwhelmed — AT&T's servers experienced widespread outages during the morning of July 11, leaving many customers unable to activate their new phones for several hours. Steve Jobs reportedly called the activation problems "a mess" in a brief statement. Despite the rocky launch infrastructure, Apple sold 1 million iPhone 3G units in the first weekend — a milestone the original iPhone had taken 74 days to reach.

In the UK, where O2 offered the iPhone 3G at £99 with an 18-month contract (£45/month), lines at the Regent Street Apple Store in London and O2 stores began forming the night of July 10. The UK launch was covered live by the BBC. In Japan — one of the 22 launch countries — the iPhone 3G launched through SoftBank, and lines outside the SoftBank Ginza flagship store in Tokyo were described by local press as the longest seen since PlayStation 3 launch queues.

## The App Store Effect

The iPhone 3G's practical significance was inseparable from the [App Store](/news/science-technology/apple-app-store-launch/) that launched with it. The first 24 hours of App Store availability saw 10 million downloads across approximately 800 available titles (additional apps had cleared Apple's review process after the July 10 launch). By September 2008, the App Store catalog had grown to roughly 3,000 applications; by December 2008, over 10,000. The one-billionth App Store download — a free copy of *Tap Tap Revenge* — was logged on April 23, 2009, nine months after launch.

The economic model underpinning the App Store was straightforward but unprecedented in mobile software: developers set their own prices, retained 70% of revenue, and received access to a global distribution channel with no need to negotiate with individual carriers or retailers. This arrangement empowered independent developers — a single programmer with a compelling app concept could reach millions of users — and sparked what analysts at Morgan Stanley estimated by 2009 was a $4 billion annual mobile application economy. Key early App Store successes included the navigation app *TomTom* ($99.99), game *Fieldrunners*, and the social network client *Tweetie*.

## Competitive Landscape in 2008

The iPhone 3G's launch occurred against a mobile market landscape that would transform rapidly within months. In 2008, the dominant smartphone platforms were:

- **BlackBerry** (Research In Motion): held approximately 44% of the U.S. smartphone market in Q1 2008, dominant in enterprise email. The BlackBerry Bold 9000 launched in summer 2008, adding 3G and Wi-Fi, but the BlackBerry's software ecosystem remained carrier- and enterprise-controlled.
- **Windows Mobile 6.1** (Microsoft): used by HTC, Motorola, and other OEMs; characterized by stylus-based interfaces and complex configuration.
- **Symbian** (Nokia): dominant globally by unit volume but largely outside the U.S. market; Nokia's N-series devices had similar hardware capabilities but lacked an accessible consumer software marketplace.
- **Android**: Google's first Android device, the [HTC Dream](/news/science-technology/htc-dream-android-launch/) (T-Mobile G1 in the U.S.), launched on September 23, 2008 — ten weeks after the iPhone 3G. Android's open-source model and carrier flexibility positioned it as the primary competitor.

Industry analysts at Gartner estimated the global smartphone market at 139 million units in 2008, of which Apple captured approximately 9.8% (13.7 million units). By 2012, Apple's iPhone and Google's Android together would account for over 80% of global smartphone shipments, having effectively eliminated competing platforms.

## Significance

The iPhone 3G was the device at which the smartphone crossed from aspirational consumer technology to mainstream computing platform. By combining 3G speeds, sub-$200 pricing, and a curated third-party application ecosystem, it demonstrated that the constraints that had limited earlier smartphones — expensive data plans, poor browsing performance, and carrier-controlled software — could be engineered away. The App Store model it launched — centralized, curated, commission-based — became the defining structure of the mobile software industry; Google's Android Market (later Google Play, launched October 22, 2008) directly emulated it.

The iPhone 3G also marked Apple's transformation from a niche computer company into a consumer electronics giant. Apple's market capitalization, which had stood at approximately $150 billion at the start of 2008, would grow more than tenfold by 2018, with the iPhone accounting for approximately 60% of revenue. The 30% commission structure that began with the App Store's 2008 launch became the subject of significant antitrust scrutiny in the early 2020s (most directly in *Epic Games v. Apple*, filed in 2020). But in July 2008, the iPhone 3G simply felt like a beginning — and it was.
