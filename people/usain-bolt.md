# Usain Bolt

**Category**: People  
**Key figures**: Usain Bolt (athlete), Glen Mills (coach), Asafa Powell, Michael Phelps (fellow Beijing record-breaker)

## Summary

Usain Bolt is a Jamaican track and field sprinter who emerged as the world's fastest human in 2008. At the Beijing Summer Olympics, between August 16 and August 22, Bolt set three world records — in the 100 metres, 200 metres, and 4×100 metres relay — sweeping three gold medals in the sprint events and establishing himself as the most dominant sprinter of his era. His performances were characterized not only by the records themselves but by the apparent ease with which they were achieved.

Born on August 21, 1986, in Sherwood Content, Trelawny, Jamaica, Bolt had shown exceptional ability as a teenage 200m specialist — he became the youngest world junior champion in 2002 at age 15 — but injuries and inconsistent conditioning delayed his senior breakthrough until he began working with coach Glen Mills in 2004. The 2008 season saw him shift primary focus to the 100m, a distance over which he broke the world record twice before the Olympics even began.

## Early Career

Bolt grew up in rural Jamaica and was introduced to competitive running through school athletics. At the 2002 World Junior Championships in Kingston, Jamaica, he won the 200m gold medal in 20.61 seconds — at 15 years and 332 days, the youngest world junior champion in the event's history. His early career was hampered by scoliosis and hamstring injuries that required careful management.

From 2004, Bolt trained under Glen Mills at the Racers Track Club in Kingston. Mills restructured his training regimen to prioritize strength and conditioning alongside technical refinement of his start — historically a weakness given Bolt's 6-foot-5-inch (1.96 m) frame, which required more strides to reach top speed than shorter rivals. By 2007 Bolt had run 19.75 seconds in the 200m and was setting senior personal bests.

In May 2008, Bolt entered the 100m at the Reebok Grand Prix in New York City on May 31 and ran 9.72 seconds, breaking Asafa Powell's world record of 9.74 seconds set in 2007. It was only the seventh time he had competed professionally at 100m. The performance signaled that Beijing would witness something unprecedented.

## Beijing 2008 Performances

**100 metres final — August 16, 2008**: Bolt won the Olympic final in 9.69 seconds, improving his own world record by 0.03 seconds. Unusually, he began celebrating with approximately 20 metres remaining — spreading his arms wide and beating his chest — before crossing the finish line. Despite visibly easing off at the end of the race with one shoelace untied, he still broke the world record by a margin that prompted analysts to suggest times below 9.60 seconds were achievable. The second-place finisher, Richard Thompson of Trinidad and Tobago (9.89 seconds), ran what would have been the world record in any prior Olympic Games.

**200 metres final — August 20, 2008**: Bolt won in 19.30 seconds, breaking Michael Johnson's 12-year-old world record of 19.32 seconds set at the 1996 Atlanta Olympics. He also broke Johnson's Olympic record of 19.32 seconds. The time represented the largest single improvement of the 200m world record since electronic timing was introduced.

**4×100 metres relay — August 22, 2008**: Running the third leg, Bolt was part of a Jamaican team (Nesta Carter, Michael Frater, Bolt, Asafa Powell on the anchor leg) that finished in 37.10 seconds, a world record by 0.30 seconds. The relay gold completed Bolt's sweep of the three sprint events, making him the first athlete in Olympic history to set world records in all three sprint distances at a single Games. (This relay record was later disqualified by the IOC in 2017 when Nesta Carter tested positive for methylhexaneamine in a re-analysis of stored samples, stripping the team's gold medal — the 100m and 200m individual records and medals were unaffected.)

## Significance

Usain Bolt's performances at the 2008 Beijing Olympics represented a watershed moment in the history of athletics. His world records — two of them achieved while he was visibly coasting — challenged prevailing assumptions about the physiological ceiling of human sprinting speed and generated international debate about what times were ultimately achievable.

Bolt's three-event sweep was the most spectacular individual achievement of the Beijing Games, competing for public attention with Michael Phelps's eight swimming gold medals in the pool. Together, their performances defined Beijing as one of the most athletically extraordinary Olympics in the modern era.

The records and their manner of achievement — especially the 100m celebration that preceded the finish line — established Bolt as a global sports personality of rare magnitude. His success brought sustained mainstream attention to track and field events that had seen declining broadcast audiences in the preceding decade. Bolt continued his dominance through three Olympic cycles, winning a total of eight Olympic gold medals and eleven World Championships titles before retiring in 2017, cementing his status as the sport's most decorated sprinter.
