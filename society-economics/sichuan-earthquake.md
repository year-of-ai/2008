# 2008 Sichuan Earthquake

**Category:** Society & Economics

**Key Figures:** Wen Jiabao (Premier of China, who led the on-site disaster response), Hu Jintao (President and General Secretary of the Communist Party of China)

## Summary

The 2008 Sichuan earthquake, also known as the Great Sichuan or Wenchuan earthquake, struck the mountainous region of western Sichuan province, China, at 14:28 China Standard Time on May 12, 2008. With a magnitude of 8.0 Ms (measured by the China Earthquake Administration) — assessed at 7.9 Mw by the United States Geological Survey — and a shallow focal depth of about 19 kilometres, it ruptured the Longmenshan fault zone along the eastern edge of the Tibetan Plateau. The epicentre lay in Wenchuan County, roughly 80 kilometres west-northwest of the provincial capital, Chengdu, and the shaking was felt across much of China and as far away as Beijing, Shanghai, and neighbouring countries.

It was the deadliest and most destructive earthquake to strike China since the 1976 Tangshan earthquake. Official figures counted approximately 69,000 people confirmed dead, more than 18,000 missing, and over 374,000 injured, with damage spread across 58 counties and millions of survivors left homeless. The collapse of thousands of school buildings, while many nearby government and commercial structures remained standing, provoked a national controversy over shoddy "tofu-dreg" construction and official accountability. Coming just ten days after Cyclone Nargis devastated Myanmar, the disaster prompted one of the largest relief mobilisations in modern Chinese history and an unusually open initial response from the state.

## Key Facts

- **Date and time:** May 12, 2008, at 14:28:01 China Standard Time (06:28:01 UTC)
- **Epicentre:** Wenchuan County, Ngawa (Aba) Tibetan and Qiang Autonomous Prefecture, Sichuan province — about 80 km west-northwest of Chengdu
- **Magnitude:** 8.0 Ms (China Earthquake Administration); 7.9 Mw (USGS)
- **Depth:** approximately 19 km (12 miles) — a shallow rupture along the Longmenshan fault
- **Death toll:** about 69,000 confirmed dead (the great majority in Sichuan), more than 18,000 missing, and over 374,000 injured
- **Displacement:** an estimated 5 million to as many as 11 million people left homeless
- **School collapses:** more than 7,000 schoolrooms collapsed; an official tally attributed 5,335 student deaths to the disaster
- **Aftershocks:** tens of thousands recorded in the following months, including several exceeding magnitude 6.0
- **Quake lakes:** 34 "barrier lakes" formed by landslide-dammed rivers, the largest at Tangjiashan, forcing the evacuation of more than 200,000 people from downstream Mianyang
- **Economic damage:** estimated at about 845 billion yuan (roughly US$130 billion); the government later committed about 1 trillion yuan to reconstruction

## Geological Background

The earthquake occurred on the Longmenshan thrust fault, which marks the boundary between the Sichuan Basin and the eastern margin of the Tibetan Plateau. The slow collision of the Indian and Eurasian plates drives the uplift of the plateau, and the resulting compression is accommodated in part along the Longmenshan system. The 2008 rupture propagated for more than 240 kilometres along the fault, producing intense ground motion over a wide area. The combination of a shallow focus, a long surface rupture, and steep, geologically unstable terrain triggered tens of thousands of landslides that buried villages, blocked rivers, and severed roads — greatly complicating rescue access to the worst-hit valleys.

## Relief and Government Response

The Chinese government's reaction was rapid and highly visible. Premier Wen Jiabao flew to the disaster zone within hours of the quake to direct operations, and the People's Liberation Army and armed police deployed tens of thousands of troops — eventually well over 100,000 personnel — into the affected region, often marching or parachuting into valleys cut off by landslides. The state declared three days of national mourning beginning June 18, the first such observance for a natural disaster in the history of the People's Republic, during which flags flew at half-staff and public entertainment was suspended.

In contrast to the secrecy that had surrounded earlier Chinese disasters, the early response featured comparatively open domestic media coverage and a large outpouring of public donations and volunteering. The newly formed quake lakes posed a prolonged secondary threat: engineers worked for weeks to drain the unstable Tangjiashan lake by cutting a sluice channel, averting a catastrophic downstream flood. Reconstruction was organised through a "counterpart support" programme in which wealthier provinces and municipalities were each paired with a devastated county to fund and rebuild it.

## The "Tofu-Dreg" Schools Controversy

The disproportionate collapse of school buildings became the most politically sensitive aspect of the disaster. Thousands of classrooms pancaked while sturdier nearby structures survived, and grieving parents alleged that corruption and corner-cutting had produced dangerously substandard "tofu-dreg" (doufuzha) construction. The campaign for accountability — pressed by parents and by activists such as the artist Ai Weiwei, who compiled a citizen list of student victims — was met with official resistance, detentions, and restrictions on reporting. The episode came to symbolise the tension between the state's emergency competence and its intolerance of independent scrutiny.

## Significance

**A landmark national disaster** — As the deadliest earthquake in China since Tangshan in 1976, the Wenchuan earthquake reshaped Chinese disaster preparedness, building codes, and emergency-response doctrine, and prompted major investment in seismic monitoring and reconstruction.

**A moment of relative openness** — The visible leadership of Premier Wen Jiabao, the unprecedented national mourning, and the comparatively candid early coverage marked a brief departure from official secrecy and showcased the state's mobilisation capacity months before the Beijing Olympics.

**A catalyst for civic accountability** — The schools controversy galvanised parents, journalists, and activists to demand transparency over construction standards and casualty figures, testing the boundaries of civil society in China and drawing international attention to official accountability.

**One of two great May 2008 disasters in Asia** — Striking only ten days after Cyclone Nargis hit Myanmar, the earthquake made May 2008 one of the deadliest months for natural disasters in recent Asian history, and the divergent state responses — China's swift mobilisation versus the Burmese junta's obstruction — became a frequent point of comparison.
